Preventive Care Guidelines

Visiting the Doctor

Regular doctor checkups and screenings are important in keeping yourself and your family in good health. BCBSNC has compiled the following health guidelines for family members of all ages.

Check your #parse( "members/secure/benefits/include_benefits_booklet.htm" ) for your specific preventive health benefits.

Babies and Toddlers (0-35 months)

Your baby should visit the doctor for regular check-ups at the following ages:

  • 2-4 days
  • 2 weeks to 1 month
  • 2 months
  • 4 months
  • 6 months
  • 9 months
  • 12 months
  • 15 months
  • 18 months
  • 24 months
  • 30 months

The doctor will conduct routine screenings and tests including:

  • Length and weight
  • Physical exam
  • Developmental and behavioral assessment
  • Hemoglobin (12 months to test for anemia)
  • Hearing

Your doctor may also ask about the following issues:

  • Diet
  • Injury prevention
  • Car seat use
  • Dental health
  • Effects of second hand smoke

If your doctor does not ask about any of these health issues, you should bring them up if you have questions or concerns.

Children and Adolescents (3 to 18 years)

Children ages 3 to 18 should visit their pediatrician for a routine check-up each year. Routine screening and tests include:

  • Height and weight (screening for healthy weight)
  • Blood pressure
  • Vision screening
  • Hearing
  • Developmental and behavioral assessment
  • Depression screening from age 11
  • Chlamydia and gonorrhea tests for sexually active girls
  • HIV screening for adolescents ages 15-18
  • Physical exam

Download the Infants and Children (0-6 years) Vaccine Schedule: CDC Website

Download the Children and Adolescents (7-18 years) Vaccine Schedule: CDC Website

Your doctor may also ask about the following issues:

  • Nutrition
  • Exercise
  • Accident prevention (seatbelt or car seat use, helmets, etc.)
  • Smoking, drug use, and safe sex practices

If your doctor does not bring up any of these health issues, you should ask about them if you have questions or concerns.

Adults (19+ years)

Adults ages 19+ should visit their doctor for a routine check-up every one to three years and after 65+ annually.

Vaccines

Download the Adult Vaccine Schedule: CDC Website

  • Tetanus, Diphtheria, pertussis (whooping cough) vaccine (Tdap vaccine once, 10 years after your last tetanus followed by Td every 10 years)
  • Influenza (Flu) vaccine (annually)
  • Hepatitis B vaccine (adults at risk)
  • Measles-Mumps-Rubella (MMR) vaccine (adults who've never received vaccine)
  • Meningococcal vaccine (college students in dormitories)
  • Varicella (chickenpox) vaccine (adults who've never had chicken pox and never received vaccine)
  • Human papillomavirus or HPV (protects against cervical cancer and genital warts) (adult women ages 19-26)
  • Pneumococcal vaccine (adults at risk)
  • Zoster (shingles) vaccine (adults 50+, one dose)3

BCBSNC provides these guidelines to help you understand how to best manage your health.

These are general health guidelines; your doctor may have more specific advice for you. These guidelines were written based on recommendations from national organizations, including the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force, American Academy of Pediatrics, and American Academy of Family Physicians. Our guidelines are updated periodically as new medical advances are made in preventive health. To minimize out-of-pocket costs, visit an in-network provider for your preventive care. These guidelines are subject to the limitations of each member's preventive care benefits, so check your benefit booklet for details about your specific preventive care benefits. Services that are not delivered in an in-network, office-based setting or stand alone clinic filing as an office visit and services that include additional procedures or diagnostic services may be subject to your copay, deductible and coinsurance.
